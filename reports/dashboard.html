What is this?

What the model does

It reads each incoming vehicle claim and gives it a suspicion score between 0 and 1. Claims scoring below the cut-off (0.55) are approved automatically; claims at or above it are flagged for a human adjuster to review before any money is paid. The model never rejects a claim on its own - it only decides who gets a second look.

How it does it

The model learned from 750 past claims whose outcome (valid or invalid) is already known. It looks at patterns across the whole claim: the size and make-up of the amounts claimed, how the claim compares to the policy's premium and deductible, vehicle age, customer tenure, incident circumstances, and whether supporting evidence (like a police report) was provided. No single detail decides - it is the combination that raises or lowers the score.

What is being evaluated here

Everything on this page is measured on 250 claims the model never saw during training - a held-back sample that simulates brand-new claims arriving tomorrow. That makes these numbers an honest preview of real-world performance, not a memory test.

Why these metrics matter

Two mistakes cost money in opposite ways: missing an invalid claim means paying it in full, while flagging a valid one wastes $500 of adjuster time and delays an honest customer. The metrics below track both sides - how much fraud is caught, how much review effort that takes, and the net dollar result - because a model is only useful if the savings outweigh the review cost and the workload stays within the team's 30% capacity.

Why the cut-off is set at 0.55

Moving the cut-off changes total cost: too lenient wastes money on unnecessary reviews, too strict lets fraud through. The line shows total cost at every possible cut-off on the test claims; the orange marker is the value chosen during training (within the 30% review-capacity limit).

Ranking quality (for the technically curious)

This ROC curve shows the trade-off between catching invalid claims and wrongly flagging valid ones, across all possible cut-offs. The further the curve bows above the dashed diagonal (random guessing), the better. Area under the curve: 0.776; precision-recall AUC: 0.551 vs a 0.25 baseline.
